What Are You, Little Neutron Star?

Millions of neutron stars populate our galaxy, the milky way. Made almost entirely of neutrons, these stellar corpses are so dense that a ‘sugar cube’ sized object of the neutron star material would weigh as much as all of the people on earth. A neutron star is the dense core left behind of a massive star.

A neutron star does not generate any light or heat of its own after its formation. Over millions of years its latent heat will gradually cool from an initial 600,000 degrees Kelvin, eventually ending its life as the cold, dead remnant of a once-glorious star.

There are estimated to be more than a hundred million neutron stars in our Milky Way galaxy. However, many will be old and cold, and therefore difficult to detect. Before we look into how neutron stars form, let’s see where and under which circumstance these appear in the life cycle of stars.

Neutron Stars - Origin Story

WHICH STARS FORM NEUTRON STARS?

The stars that eventually become neutron stars start out with about 8 to 20-30 solar masses. Stars with masses much less than 8 solar masses become white dwarfs, whereas those with masses a lot higher than 20-30 solar masses collapse into black holes instead.

FORMATION OF NEUTRON STARS

Throughout much of their lives, stars maintain a delicate balancing act. Gravity tries to compress the star while the star’s internal pressure exerts an outward push. The outward pressure is caused by nuclear fusion at the star’s core. This fusion “burning” is the process by which stars shine.

In a supernova explosion, gravity suddenly and catastrophically gets the upper hand in the war it has been waging with the star’s internal pressure for millions or billions of years. With its nuclear fuel exhausted and the outward pressure removed, gravity suddenly compresses the star inward. Overall, for all solar mass type stars, the basic idea is that when the central part of the star fuses its way to iron, it can’t go any farther since iron 56 at a low pressure has the highest binding energy per nucleon of any element, so fusion or fission of iron 56 requires an energy input. Thus, the iron core just accumulates until it gets to about 1.4 solar masses. Then the electron degeneracy pressure that had been supporting the core against gravity gives up and collapses inward. A shock wave travels to the core and rebounds, blowing the star apart. This whole process takes perhaps a couple of seconds.

But gravity’s victory is not yet complete. With most of the star blown into space, the core remains, which may only possess a couple of times the mass of our sun. Gravity continues to compress it, to a point where the atoms become so compacted and so close together that electrons are violently thrust into their parent nuclei, combining with the protons to form neutrons. In total neutron stars are 90% neutrons, basically making them giant atomic nucleus.

Thus, the neutron star gets its name from its composition. Gravity has now created a superdense, neutron-rich material – called neutronium – in a city-sized sphere. Since the supernova rate is around 1 per 30 years, and because most supernovae probably make neutron stars instead of black holes, in the 10-billion-year lifetime of the galaxy there have probably been 108 to 109 neutrons stars formed.

BALANCE OF FORCES

These stars only exist because of a delicate balance of forces. On one hand you have gravity trying to collapse the star further. Yet resistance to gravitational collapse comes in the form of something called degeneracy pressure. This is generated because there comes a point when it’s too hard to squash nuclear material closer together and so the two opposing forces balance each other out.

This also indicates that the most massive neutron stars should be the smallest – more mass means stronger gravity and therefore a more compact, tightly squeezed object.

Black Hole Or Neutron Star?

Neutron stars and black holes are very similar in the way they are formed and even a lot of their core characteristics such as a strong magnetic field, overall dense presence and how black holes are produced in the same way with the only difference being the size of the star that dies to create them.

It’s more so on the physical side of things where the 2 differ significantly, from the difference in their natural state where neutron stars are solid and black holes aren’t, their temperature where black holes are almost absolute zero and neutron stars are blazing hot, the difference of brightness for obvious reasons and beyond.

A CRASH-COURSE IN BLACK HOLES

A black hole is created when a large star with a mass higher than about 20-30 solar masses collapses below its Schwarzschild radius causing a supernova explosion. The density of the star creates a massive gravitational pocket and pulls everything within its vicinity into it.

The edge of a black hole is known as the event horizon and once something crosses this point, there is no escape. The gravitational pull of a black hole is so immense that even light is sucked inside. All objects pulled in are subjected to an unimaginable crushing gravitational force as they are pulled toward the super dense end point of the black hole known as the singularity.

These are among the most powerful entities in our universe so despite their differences they aren’t all that different from each other.

SIMILARITIES

  • are created after a star dies violently via a supernova explosion

  • are extremely dense entities

  • both have very strong magnetic fields

  • have an extremely strong gravitational pull

  • both entities warp spacetime and matter around them

DIFFERENCES

  • black holes are much larger in mass than neutron stars

  • neutron stars are significantly brighter than black holes

  • these stars are virtually the densest solid matter in space whilst black holes aren’t solid objects

  • black holes trap all the surrounding light that crosses its vicinity

  • neutron stars are made of compactly packed together neutrons whilst we don’t know what components are used to make black holes besides the explosion necessary to form them

  • black holes are extremely cold near its centre, very close to absolute zero, whilst neutron stars are very hot

  • neutron stars pulsate or flicker at regular intervals

Anatomy

The exact structure of a neutron star to this day is unclear. Current thinking suggests that the star possesses a thin crust of iron, perhaps a mile or so thick. Under that, the composition is largely neutrons, taking various forms the further down in the neutron star they are. As they resemble different types of pasta, this structure has been named nuclear pasta.

NUCLEAR PASTA

Near the surface of the neutron star, this nuclear pasta takes the form of bubbles called gnocchi. Go deeper and the growing pressure compacts the pasta into a shape more like spaghetti, followed by compressed flat sheets of lasagne.

After this structure compacts even further the deeper we go, the tables turn and going backwards we find the ‘anti-spaghetti’ stage, a partially filled structure with spaghetti sized holes. Going deeper the holes become smaller and fewer, we’re near the end at the anti-gnocchi stage.

The core of the neutron star may consist of a soup of quarks.

The layers of nuclear pasta in a typical neutron star are about 100m thick, still weighing more than 3,000 earths. But it is also thought to be unstable, meaning it could produce ripples in space called gravitational waves.

Scientists have not backed this theory up yet, making nuclear pasta remain hypothetical.

The atmosphere of a neutron star comprises hydrogen, helium and carbon. The outer crust contains ions and electrons, while the inner crust contains ions and superfluid neutrons. The outer core is superconducting protons, but what exactly comprises the inner core? That fact remains unknown.

Characterisations Of Neutron Stars

Because these stars are so dense, they also have intense gravitational fields and the strongest magnetic fields in the known universe.

The gravity of a neutron star is about a thousand billion times stronger than that of the earths. Thus, the surface of those is exceedingly smooth, gravity does not permit anything tall to exist. Neutron stars are thought to have “mountains”, but they are only inches tall.

On a neutron star, intense magnetic fields focus radio waves into two beams firing into space from its magnetic poles.

MAGNETAR

A neutron star that has an abnormally strong magnetic field is known as a magnetar, able to pull the keys out of your pocket from as far away as the moon. Their magnetic field intensity is about 100 thousand million Tesla, a thousand times more than an ordinary neutron star. In a magnetar, with its huge magnetic field, movements in the crust cause the neutron star to release a vast amount of energy in the form of electromagnetic radiation. A magnetar called SGR 1806-20 had a burst where in one-tenth of a second it released more energy than the sun has emitted in the last 100.000 years. To this day the origin of magnetars is not well understood.

PULSAR

Some of these stars rotate extremely rapidly and are the fastest spinning stars in the universe. They are called “pulsars” as they generate regular pulses of electromagnetic radiation including radio, visible, X-ray and gamma-ray wavelengths. These pulses are often compared to a spinning lighthouse beacon that appears to flash on and off. We can use the radio beams of a pulsar to measure just how fast they are. The fastest-rotating neutron star yet discovered rotates an incredible 716 times per second, which is about a quarter of the speed of light.

Neutron stars, including magnetars and pulsars, are thought to be responsible for several phenomena, including the mysterious Fast Radio Bursts and the so-called Soft Gamma Repeaters.
